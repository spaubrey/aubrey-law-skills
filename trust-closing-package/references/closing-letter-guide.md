## Section 2: Why Trust Funding Matters

"Your Revocable Living Trust is the cornerstone of your estate plan. However, a trust can only control assets that are actually titled in its name (or that name the Trust as a beneficiary). Assets that remain in your individual name at death may be subject to probate — the very process your Trust is designed to avoid.

Funding your Trust is the final — and most important — step. The checklist below identifies the most common asset categories and the action required for each."

---

## Section 3: Trust Funding Checklist

Intro: "Work through each category below and check off each item as you complete it. Please contact our office with any questions about a specific asset."

**Table formatting:**
- 3 columns: ☐ checkbox (narrow), Asset/Account Type (medium), Action Required (wide)
- Header row: shaded, white text, teal background (`0F6E56`)
- Body rows: alternating or plain white
- Full-width table (9360 DXA for 0.7in side margins), borders `CCCCCC`
- Asset/Account names: bold

**Standard rows to include** (always, unless Scott flags a row to suppress):

| Asset/Account Type | Action Required |
|---|---|
| Real Estate | Record a new deed transferring the property into the name of your Trust. Contact a real estate attorney or our office for assistance with deed preparation and Registry filing. |
| Bank Accounts | Visit your bank branch and retitle the account in the name of your Trust. Bring a copy of your Certificate of Trust. |
| Investment / Brokerage Accounts | Contact your brokerage firm and request that the account be retitled in the name of your Trust. A Certificate of Trust or Trust Certification is typically required. |
| Certificates of Deposit (CDs) | When each CD matures, retitle it into the name of your Trust. Early retitling may trigger penalties — wait for maturity when possible. |
| Business Interests (LLC / Corp.) | Consult with your business attorney to transfer membership interests or shares into the Trust. Operating agreements or bylaws may require consent. |
| Life Insurance | You may designate your Trust as the primary or contingent beneficiary. Contact your insurance provider for a beneficiary change form. |
| Retirement Accounts (IRA, 401(k), 403(b)) | **Do NOT transfer retirement accounts into the Trust.** Instead, name your spouse (if applicable) as primary beneficiary and your Trust or chosen individuals as contingent beneficiaries. |
| Vehicles (Cars, Boats, RVs) | In Massachusetts, transferring vehicles into a trust is generally not recommended due to insurance and registry complications. Leave vehicles in your individual name; your Pour-Over Will serves as a backstop. |
| Tangible Personal Property | The Assignment of Personal Property included in your package transfers your household goods and personal effects to the Trust automatically — no additional action is required. |
| Safe Deposit Box | Contact your bank to retitle access to the safe deposit box in the name of the Trust. |
| Notes / Mortgages Receivable | Consult with our office. If someone owes you money under a written note, that note can be assigned to the Trust by endorsement. |
| Future Acquisitions | Acquire any new property directly in the name of your Trust whenever possible to avoid the need for future transfers. |

**Suppress** the Business Interests row if Scott confirms the client has no business interests. Add a note in the summary if any row was suppressed.

## Section 4: Your Certificate of Trust

"I have prepared a Certificate of Trust for your convenience. This is a short summary document — not the full trust — that you can provide to banks, brokerages, title companies, and other institutions when retitling assets. It confirms the existence and key terms of your Trust without disclosing all of its private provisions. Institutions may also request their own internal forms, which is perfectly normal. Please call our office if any institution raises an objection or asks for documentation we have not already provided."

## Section 5: Beneficiary Designations

"Assets with beneficiary designations — such as retirement accounts, life insurance, and annuities — pass outside of your Trust and are governed by whoever is named as beneficiary. Please review and update your designations as follows:"

Bullet list:
- **Retirement accounts (IRA, 401(k), 403(b)):** Name your spouse as primary beneficiary (if married); name your intended beneficiaries or your Trust as contingent beneficiary. Contact your plan administrator for forms.
- **Life insurance:** Review whether the Trust should be named as a beneficiary. In many cases the Trust is appropriate; in others, naming individuals directly is preferable. Call our office for guidance.
- **Transfer-on-Death / Payable-on-Death accounts:** Review with your bank or brokerage to ensure designations are consistent with your overall plan.

---

## Section 6: Keeping Your Plan Current

"Your estate plan reflects your wishes, your family, and your assets as they exist today. Life changes — and your plan should keep pace. Please contact our office to review your documents when:"

Bullet list:
- You marry, divorce, or separate
- A beneficiary, trustee, or agent predeceases you or becomes incapacitated
- You have or adopt a child or grandchild
- You acquire or sell significant assets, especially real property
- You move to another state
- There is a significant change in tax law
- Your wishes or family circumstances change in any material way

**If minor children:** Add this bullet: "Your minor children reach adulthood and you wish to update guardianship designations or distribution provisions."

Closing note: "As a general practice, I recommend reviewing your plan every three to five years even if no major life event has occurred."
